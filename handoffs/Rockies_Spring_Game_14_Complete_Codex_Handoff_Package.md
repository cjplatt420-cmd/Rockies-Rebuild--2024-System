## 6. ORGANIZATIONAL TRENDS

### Trend 1 — Fastball-family command is ahead of breaking-ball command

Quantrill’s two-seam/cutter combination, Hollowell’s fastball/sinker/cutter group, Bird’s sinker, Mejía’s four-seamer, and Bunning’s two-seam/sinker all produced usable or strong strike rates. The repeated weakness was the second breaking pitch: Bunning’s slider at 45%, Bird’s slurve at 40%, Hollowell’s slider at 33%, and both Mejía secondaries at 50%.

**Development response:** Pitchers should not merely throw more fastballs. They should receive targeted work on landing one secondary for a strike and using it as a chase pitch, because major-league hitters will otherwise sit on the better-commanded fastball.

### Trend 2 — Strike percentage and effective command are not the same

Vodnik is the clearest example. Even with the reported 82% total, he allowed a 106 mph double and a two-run home run. Bunning’s corrected component rate is approximately 66%, yet he allowed five runs.

**Development response:** Evaluate location, count, and contact quality alongside raw strike rate. Middle-zone strikes that create extra-base damage are not positive command.

### Trend 3 — Colorado has individual offensive tools but insufficient lineup continuity

The Rockies had six meaningful hard-contact or premium-opponent markers, but those events were separated by strikeouts, routine outs, and empty innings.

**Development response:** Build evaluation lineups that place high-contact/on-base candidates ahead of the best damage bats. In remaining spring games, give Tovar, Amador, Jones, Martinez, and McMahon overlapping innings rather than scattering their strongest at-bats across substitutions.
